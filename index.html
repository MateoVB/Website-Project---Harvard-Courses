Welcome to Life After CS50!

CS@Harvard is a course selection tool designed to assist Harvard undergraduates in their journey through CS following CS50. CS50 provides the fundamental building blocks for all CS courses at Harvard in promoting computational thinking and introducing students to basic programming languages starting with C. Moreover, CS50 is widely renowned as the course one takes as an introduction to CS at Harvard. All of this has made CS50 one of the most enrolled courses at Harvard. Consequently, it became incumbent on us to provide a service to the many CS50 graduates that assists their search for more advanced CS courses. Our website allows students to filter and compare classes based on course ratings, professor reviews, average weekly workload, and other criteria to best optimise their choices.



How to Use CS@Harvard

To browse through all CS courses aimed at undergraduates at Harvard, select the "All" tab on the top navigation bar. There, you can scroll through and click on any course listed. To filter through courses based on the semester in which it is offered, average weekly workload, and more, select the "Search" tab above. To compare and contrast the data on two courses, select the "Compare" tab above. Finally, to learn more about CS@Harvard and its creators, go to the "About" page.



How to Interpret Our Data

  • Total Students: Provides user with a rough estimate of class size. May be of importance if the user wishes to have a range of small and large classes for their next semester.
  • Proportion of Undergraduates: Reflection of how many undergraduates to graduates taking the course.
  • Workload: The average number of hours outside of lessons students spend on the course.
  • Course Score: A general summary out of 5 on the quality of the materials, assignments, feedback and section for each course.
  • Instructor Score: A general summary out of 5 on how informative or useful the instructor is in terms of giving effective lectures or presentations, being accessible outside of class, facilitating discussion and encouraging participation, giving useful feedback and returning assignments in a timely fashion.
  • Recommend: To what extent would graduates of the course recommend that others take it the next time that it is offered?



Disclaimer

The figures provided for each course are based on student evaluations and college enrolment statistics for the latest semester in which that course was offered. Since then, courses may have changed such that the next time they are offered they may have different instructors, problem sets and/or syllabus content. Updates on any changes to courses will be appear on the website as soon as we receive notice, but they will be by no means comprehensive. It is ultimately up to users to determine to what extent our data can reliably predict course outcomes in the future.
